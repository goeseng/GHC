Good Hands Club

A Korean adaptation of the Men's Sheds model — a task-based community for retired men in their sixties.

Rebuilding a sense of role, self-efficacy and connection through cooking, repair, and craft. Because shoulder to shoulder, life-skills become a way back into community.

#1
Highest suicide rate in the OECDMiddle-aged and older men are especially at risk in Korea. As roughly 3.9 million second-wave baby boomers (born 1968–1974) enter retirement, male isolation is becoming a serious public concern.
01 · The Problem

Retirement begins with the loss of a role.

When work ends, so do the responsibilities, goals, and daily tasks that came with it — along with the chances to be recognised and the networks built around a career. Left unaddressed, this becomes more than a personal problem.

01

Loss of role

With work gone, so are the responsibilities, targets, and daily tasks. The sense of being needed quietly fades.

02

Absence of recognition

Salaries, titles, and colleague feedback disappear. Decades of experience and skill no longer find an audience.

03

Shrinking networks

Work-centred relationships fall away, but new social spaces — especially ones that welcome older men — are hard to find.

Existing welfare programmes hit a wall of pride. For task-oriented men, "friendship-only" or hobby-only programmes read as receiving help — which makes voluntary participation genuinely difficult. Pre-survey & interview analysis
02 · Key Insights

When role and relationship are designed together, isolation gives way.

Five findings from alumni mentoring and field interviews that shape the Good Hands Club model.

Finding 1

Framing the invitation around usefulness, not loneliness, unlocks voluntary participation.

48% of pre-survey respondents named "whether the activity will match my level" as the biggest barrier to entry. The core anxiety isn't stigma — it's competence.

Finding 2

A place worth going to, on a regular schedule.

Retirement is also the loss of one's own space. A workshop that can be visited on a rhythm generates the kind of sustained participation that changes outcomes.

Finding 3

Family and community infrastructure carry the nudge that stabilises participation.

64% consult their spouse first when deciding on new activities. Welfare centres and volunteer hubs become anchor spaces; partner-driven referral becomes part of the design.

Finding 4

The two-year mark after retirement is the critical window.

"The first year is fine. It's around the one-year point that boredom sets in," said one interviewee. We aim to reach men before that early relief turns into quiet emptiness.

Finding 5

Progression that is staged, but not hierarchical.

Workplace hierarchies re-emerge easily in male-only spaces. A horizontal three-step arc — skills, roles, contribution — keeps engagement high over time.

03 · The Model

From emptiness to contribution.

Life-skills as the entry point. From the self, outward to family, and onward to the community — usefulness expands in three concentric steps.

STEP 1
Useful to me

Entry via life-skill workshops

Rebuilding self-efficacy

The invitation is a "useful task," not a support group. Cooking, repair, woodwork — everyday skills that let a man walk in without pretence. Completing one small task restores the quiet certainty of "I can still do this."

STEP 2
Useful to family

A shared workshop with roles

Reconnecting close relationships

In an open workshop, members take on roles and answer real requests — a repair for a neighbour, a meal for family. Skills learned here find their first audience close to home, and recognition begins to flow back.

STEP 3
Useful to society

Contribution with a structure

A civic role, and a system that sustains it

Members move into roles the community actually needs — meal deliveries, elder care, community gardening, mentoring. A role stipend and a "usefulness currency" recognise contribution and keep the community turning long-term.

04 · Differentiation

A different starting point than existing programmes.

A space for everyone A space that fits retired men Community workshops are open to all — but Good Hands Club is designed so that men in their sixties feel competent and at home from the first visit.
Simple participation Recovery of a role Rather than slotting people into predefined tasks, the model surfaces each member's skills and experience and builds new roles around them.
Unpaid volunteering A structure of recognition Instead of relying on unpaid goodwill, role stipends, activity fees, and a community currency give contribution real weight and permanence.
05 · Validation

Measured, honestly, in three stages.

From entry through sustained impact, each phase is bound by specific indicators — so we know what is actually working.

Stage 1 · Entry

Short workshop

  • Enrolment conversion rate 25–30%+
  • Distribution of intake channels tracked
  • One-person, one-outcome completion 90%+
  • Intent to enrol in 8-week programme 60%+
Stage 2 · Core hypothesis

8-week main programme

  • Per-session attendance 70% / completion 70%+
  • Significant reduction on loneliness scale (K-DJGLS, p<.05)
  • Priority: change on social-loneliness subscale
  • Re-participation or peer referral intent 30%+
Stage 3 · Sustaining

Long-term impact

  • Self-organised operational roles 30%+
  • Identity shift: "I am someone who ___"
  • Community deliveries, exhibitions, or sales
  • 6-month re-measurement to confirm retention
